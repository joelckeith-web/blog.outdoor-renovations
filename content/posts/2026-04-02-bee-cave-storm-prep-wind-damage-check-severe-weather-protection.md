## What the Recent Winds Did to Your Bee Cave Landscape

Those 37 mph wind gusts didn't just rustle leaves. They loosened tree branches that are now hanging hazards, shifted mulch away from plant beds, and likely clogged your gutters with debris. In Bee Cave's Hill Country terrain, wind damage can create bigger problems when storms arrive.

Walk your property and look for these wind damage signs:

**Tree and Plant Issues:**
- Loose or cracked branches hanging over walkways or structures
- Newly exposed root systems where soil blew away
- Leaning fence posts or loose fence panels
- Mulch blown into drainage areas or away from plant beds

**Drainage and Hardscape Problems:**
- Leaves and debris in gutters, French drains, or catch basins
- Shifted gravel or decorative rock in pathways
- Loose pavers or flagstone that could become projectiles
- Soil erosion around retaining walls or slope plantings

## Critical Pre-Storm Checklist for Bee Cave Properties

You have until Thursday morning to prepare. Here's your priority action list:

**Drainage System Preparation (Priority #1):**
Clear all gutters, downspouts, and French drains of debris from the recent winds. Check that water flows freely from gutters to drainage areas. If you have a dry creek bed or bioswale, remove any accumulated leaves or branches that could create dams during heavy rain.

Test your drainage by running water through the system. In Bee Cave's alkaline limestone soil, drainage problems show up fast during heavy rain events. A system that seems fine in dry weather can fail catastrophically when storms hit.

**Irrigation System Check:**
Inspect all sprinkler heads and drip irrigation lines. Wind can shift or damage these systems, and you don't want broken irrigation adding to drainage problems during storms. Turn off automatic watering cycles - you won't need them this week, and oversaturated soil increases erosion risk.

Smart irrigation controllers automatically adjust for weather conditions. [EPA WaterSense](https://www.epa.gov/watersense) data shows these systems prevent overwatering during rain events, crucial for preventing erosion and plant disease in storm conditions.

**Secure Loose Materials:**
That decorative rock or mulch shifted by recent winds needs to be reset or temporarily contained. Loose materials become projectiles in severe weather and can clog drainage systems.

**Key Takeaway:** Drainage preparation is your highest priority. A functioning drainage system is the difference between minor inconvenience and major property damage during severe weather.

## Understanding Bee Cave's Unique Storm Challenges

Bee Cave's location in the Hill Country creates specific challenges during severe weather that differ from flatter areas of Austin Metro. The limestone bedrock and sloped terrain that make the area beautiful also make it vulnerable to flash flooding and erosion.

**Limestone Bedrock Effects:**
Central Texas limestone doesn't absorb water like other soil types. During heavy rain, water runs across the surface rather than soaking in. This creates rapid runoff that can overwhelm drainage systems and cause flash flooding in low-lying areas.

**Slope Challenges:**
Many Bee Cave properties have significant elevation changes. While these create beautiful terraced landscapes, they also concentrate water flow during storms. A small drainage problem at the top of a slope becomes a major erosion issue by the time water reaches the bottom.

**Soil Considerations:**
Above the limestone, you'll typically find either thin rocky soil in the hills or expansive clay in lower areas. Both present challenges during severe weather. Rocky soil sheds water quickly, while clay holds too much water and becomes unstable when saturated.
