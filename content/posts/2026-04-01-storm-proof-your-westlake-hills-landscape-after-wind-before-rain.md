## Wind Damage Assessment: What Those 37 MPH Gusts Left Behind

The recent wind event wasn't just a breezy afternoon. Sustained winds over 25 mph and gusts exceeding 35 mph can compromise your landscape's structural integrity in ways that aren't always obvious. Here's what Kyle Stoutenger, Licensed Landscape Contractor and owner of Outdoor Renovations, sees after wind events in Westlake Hills properties.

**Tree and Plant Damage to Check:**
- Hanging or partially detached branches, especially on mature live oaks and cedar elms
- Root exposure where shallow-rooted plants lifted slightly from soil
- Leaning ornamental trees or shrubs that may have loosened root systems
- Damaged irrigation lines where shifting plants pulled connections apart

**Hardscape Issues Wind Creates:**
- Loose coping stones on retaining walls
- Shifted decorative boulders or landscape rocks
- Compromised fence panels or gate hardware
- Separated joints in flagstone or limestone patios

The limestone caliche soil common in Westlake Hills becomes particularly problematic after wind events. When plants rock in windy conditions, they create small gaps around root systems. These gaps become express channels for water infiltration during subsequent storms, leading to rapid erosion and foundation concerns.

**Key Takeaway:** Wind damage inspection isn't just about what you can see. The underground damage to root systems and irrigation infrastructure often causes the biggest problems when storms hit.

## Immediate Storm Preparation: 48 Hours Until Heavy Rain

Thursday through Sunday brings the most challenging weather pattern: multiple rounds of thunderstorms with heavy rain potential. The [National Weather Service](https://www.weather.gov) warns that spring storm systems in Central Texas can deliver 2-4 inches of rainfall per hour during peak intensity.

**Critical Drainage System Checks:**
Your [irrigation and drainage systems](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage/westlake-hills) need immediate attention. Here's the priority checklist:

1. **Surface Drainage Inspection:** Walk your property's perimeter and verify that grading maintains 2% minimum slope away from structures. Recent winds may have shifted mulch or decorative materials that affect water flow patterns.

2. **French Drain Maintenance:** Clear all drain inlets of leaves, branches, and debris. French drains handle approximately 1 inch per hour of rainfall under ideal conditions, but clogs reduce capacity by 70% or more.

3. **Gutter and Downspout Function:** Test each downspout by running water through it. Verify that extensions direct water at least 6 feet from your foundation and haven't been displaced by wind.

4. **Catch Basin Clearing:** Remove any accumulated organic matter from yard drains and catch basins. Even small debris can create backups that force water toward hardscape and structures.

Professional contractor insight: Most homeowners don't realize that Austin's clay soil (Houston Black series) expands significantly when wet. Properties in eastern Westlake Hills with clay components can see 15-20% soil volume changes during heavy rain events. This expansion can crack drainage pipes that were already stressed by recent ground movement.

## Understanding Hill Country Storm Hydrology

Westlake Hills sits in unique terrain where limestone formations create both opportunities and challenges for drainage management. The elevation changes and rock outcrops that make this area beautiful also concentrate stormwater flow in predictable patterns.

**Natural Water Flow Patterns:**
- Water moves rapidly across limestone surfaces toward natural collection points
- Clay pockets between limestone layers create perched water tables during storms
- Steep grades common in Hill Country properties can generate sheet flow velocities exceeding safe limits

Kyle Stoutenger's 16+ years working across Austin Metro & Central Texas has shown him that effective storm preparation requires understanding your specific microclimate. Properties on the western slopes of Westlake Hills shed water quickly but can overwhelm downstream drainage. Properties in natural swales collect water from multiple directions and need robust internal drainage systems.

**Key Takeaway:** Your property's position in the local watershed determines your storm risk profile. Upslope properties need erosion control; downslope properties need flood prevention.

## Protecting Landscape Investments During Severe Weather

Quality landscaping provides 100-200% return on investment, but only when it survives Central Texas weather extremes. [Property management services](https://outdoorreno.com/services/property-management/westlake-hills) include proactive storm preparation that protects your investment year-round.

**Plant Protection Strategies:**
- Stake newly planted trees and large shrubs until root systems establish
- Apply 3-4 inch mulch depth around plants to prevent soil erosion (more than 4 inches promotes crown rot in native species)
- Prune strategically to reduce wind resistance while maintaining plant health
- Install temporary wind barriers for sensitive plantings during storm season

**Hardscape Preservation:**
- Seal natural stone surfaces before storm season to prevent water infiltration
- Verify that retaining wall weep holes remain clear for proper drainage
- Apply protective treatments to metal fabrication elements exposed to wind-driven rain

Professional tip: The alkaline limestone soil common throughout Westlake Hills has a pH between 7.8-8.4, which affects how quickly organic mulches decompose. Cedar mulch lasts 18-24 months in these conditions, while hardwood mulches break down in 12-15 months. Plan mulch replacement timing around storm seasons for maximum protection.

**Key Takeaway:** Storm preparation isn't just about emergency response—it's about building resilient landscape systems that perform reliably through multiple weather cycles.
