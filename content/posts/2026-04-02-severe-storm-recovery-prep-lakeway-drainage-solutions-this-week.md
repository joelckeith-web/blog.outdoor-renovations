## What Just Happened: 37 MPH Winds and Hidden Damage

The 37 mph wind gusts that hit Lakeway over the past two days might not have made headlines, but they created conditions that will cause problems when the heavy rains arrive. Winds above 35 mph are strong enough to shift paver joints, loosen retaining wall caps, and blow debris into drainage channels.

Here's what likely happened on your property during those wind gusts:

**Hardscape Displacement**: Paver stones and flagstone can shift when wind gets underneath loose edges. Even small gaps allow wind to create uplift, especially on elevated patios and walkways common in Lakeway's hillside lots.

**Drainage Blockages**: Tree branches, leaves, and outdoor furniture debris get blown into French drains, channel drains, and gutters. A single large branch across a drainage swale can redirect water flow and create erosion channels.

**Irrigation System Misalignment**: Sprinkler heads get knocked off target, and drip irrigation lines can be pulled loose from their anchors. This isn't just about watering efficiency – misaligned irrigation creates wet spots that turn into erosion zones during heavy rain.

## What to Check Right Now: Storm Damage Assessment

Before Thursday's thunderstorms arrive, walk your property and look for these specific wind damage indicators:

**Drainage System Inspection**: Check all visible drains for debris accumulation. French drains should have clear gravel surfaces, not packed leaves or branches. Channel drains need open grates – even partial blockages reduce capacity by 60% or more.

**Hardscape Stability**: Press down on paver edges and flagstone corners. Loose stones will rock or shift under pressure. Check retaining wall caps for movement – these often get displaced first during wind events.

**Grading and Erosion**: Look for small channels or depressions that weren't there before the wind. These indicate that surface water flow patterns have changed, usually due to debris accumulation or soil displacement.

**Tree and Plant Damage**: Remove any broken branches immediately, especially those hanging over structures or drainage areas. Dead or damaged vegetation becomes projectiles in the next storm.

## Immediate Storm Preparation: Emergency Drainage Solutions

With severe weather approaching, focus on these high-impact preparations:

**Clear All Drainage Pathways**: Remove debris from gutters, downspouts, French drains, and surface channels. A single clogged drain can back up water across entire sections of your property.

**Secure Loose Materials**: Wind-shifted pavers, loose mulch, and outdoor furniture become debris that clogs drainage systems. Temporarily remove or secure anything that could move in 40+ mph winds.

**Test Drainage Flow**: Run water from a garden hose at major drainage points to verify proper flow. If water pools or drains slowly, you have a problem that needs immediate attention before the storms arrive.

**Emergency Water Diversion**: For properties with known drainage problems, set up temporary solutions like sand bag barriers or portable pumps. These aren't permanent fixes, but they prevent catastrophic damage during emergency situations.

## Long-Term Drainage Solutions for Storm-Prone Properties

Emergency preparation helps you survive this week's storms, but Lakeway's location in the Hill Country means severe weather will return. Smart property owners invest in permanent drainage improvements that handle Central Texas's intense but brief storm events.

**French Drain Systems**: Properly designed French drains move subsurface water away from structures while allowing surface infiltration. In Lakeway's limestone terrain, we typically install drains 18-24 inches deep with 4-inch perforated pipe surrounded by #57 limestone gravel.

**Bioretention and Rain Gardens**: These systems slow water flow and reduce peak runoff rates. Native plants like flame acanthus and Lindheimer muhly thrive in bioretention areas while providing 40-60% runoff reduction compared to turf grass.

**Permeable Hardscaping**: Permeable pavers and porous concrete allow water infiltration while maintaining usable outdoor space. These systems can handle rainfall rates up to 3 inches per hour – sufficient for most Central Texas storm events.

**Grade Modification**: Strategic regrading directs water away from structures and into designated drainage areas. This requires understanding soil types and existing drainage patterns – work that demands professional expertise.

The [EPA WaterSense](https://www.epa.gov/watersense) guidelines recommend drainage systems that can handle the 10-year storm event (approximately 5 inches of rain in 24 hours for Central Texas). Our designs typically exceed this standard because Hill Country properties face additional challenges from steep grades and limestone soil conditions.

> **Key Takeaway**: Permanent drainage improvements provide 24/7 protection and increase property values by 15-25% according to landscape industry data.

## Smart Irrigation During Storm Season

Heavy rain events require irrigation system adjustments to prevent overwatering and soil saturation. Smart controllers automatically pause irrigation when rainfall exceeds preset thresholds, but manual systems need attention.

**Rain Sensor Installation**: Basic rain sensors cost $30-50 and prevent irrigation systems from running during or immediately after rainfall. More advanced soil moisture sensors provide precise control based on actual soil conditions rather than just surface precipitation.

**Drainage Integration**: Irrigation zones should align with natural drainage patterns. Areas that collect runoff need different watering schedules than well-drained slopes. Overwatering low-lying areas during storm season promotes fungal diseases and root rot in native plants.

**System Vulnerability Assessment**: Check irrigation lines for exposure after wind events. Buried lines can be damaged by soil movement, and above-ground components get knocked loose. Damaged irrigation creates wet spots that become erosion zones during heavy rain.

Kyle Stoutenger's experience across 200+ Central Texas projects shows that integrated drainage and irrigation design reduces water usage by 30-40% while improving plant health during extreme weather events. "You can't treat drainage and irrigation as separate systems," he explains. "They work together, and storm events test both simultaneously."

## Hardscape Protection and Storm Recovery

Wind and rain damage hardscape installations differently than soft landscaping, but the repairs often require immediate attention to prevent further deterioration.

**Retaining Wall Drainage**: Retaining walls need proper drainage behind them to prevent hydrostatic pressure buildup. Clogged wall drains can cause structural failure during heavy rain events. Check weep holes and drainage pipes for blockages.

**Paver and Flagstone Stability**: Wind-shifted stones create entry points for water penetration. Once water gets underneath hardscapes, freeze-thaw cycles and root intrusion accelerate damage. Reset loose stones immediately after wind events.

**Joint Sand Replacement**: Wind and rain erode joint sand between pavers. Lost joint sand allows weed growth and stone movement. Polymeric sand provides better wind resistance than regular mason sand, especially in exposed locations.

## Weather-Resistant Plant Selection and Care

Storm season impacts plant materials differently based on species selection and establishment. Native plants generally handle Central Texas weather better than non-adapted species, but even natives need proper care during extreme events.

**Native Plant Storm Tolerance**: Texas sage, cedar elm, and live oak develop deep root systems that provide wind resistance. Shallow-rooted exotics like Bradford pears are notorious for storm damage and should be avoided in exposed locations.

**Proper Plant Spacing**: Overcrowded plantings create wind resistance that increases blow-over risk. Native plants need space for proper root development – typically 1.5-2 times their mature canopy width for optimal storm tolerance.

**Mulching for Storm Protection**: Proper mulch application (3-4 inches deep) protects root systems from temperature extremes and reduces soil erosion during heavy rain. Avoid mulch volcanoes against tree trunks – these promote crown rot and reduce storm tolerance.
