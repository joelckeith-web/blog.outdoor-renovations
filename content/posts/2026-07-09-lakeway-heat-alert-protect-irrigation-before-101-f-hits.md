## Why This Week's Heat Hits Lakeway Landscapes So Hard

Lakeway sits on Hill Country terrain with shallow, alkaline limestone soil, sometimes called caliche. This soil type doesn't hold water the way deeper soils do. When temps climb past 100°F, that thin soil layer heats up fast and dries out even faster.

Add in low wind speeds this week, mostly 0 to 10 mph, and you get stagnant, heavy heat sitting right on top of your lawn and beds. There's little air movement to cool the soil surface between watering cycles. That means your irrigation system has to work smarter, not just longer.

Kyle Stoutenger, Owner and Licensed Landscape Contractor at Outdoor Renovations, has watched this pattern play out for over 16 years across Austin Metro and Central Texas. "The mistake I see every July is people cranking up watering time instead of checking watering efficiency," Kyle says. "More minutes on a broken system just wastes water. A five-minute inspection can save a whole flower bed."

**Key Takeaway:** Shallow limestone soil in Lakeway heats up and dries out fast during 100°F+ stretches. Low wind this week traps heat at ground level, making irrigation efficiency more important than irrigation duration.

## Irrigation Checks to Do Before Friday

Before the heat peaks today and stays elevated through the weekend, walk your property and check these five things.

**1. Test every zone manually.** Run each irrigation zone for two minutes and watch the spray pattern. Look for clogged nozzles, heads that don't pop up fully, or dry spots that don't get covered. Heat and mineral buildup from Hill Country water can clog small drip emitters over a single season.

**2. Check your smart controller settings.** If you have a smart irrigation controller, confirm it's using current weather data. According to [EPA WaterSense](https://www.epa.gov/watersense), smart controllers can cut outdoor water use by 30-50% compared to standard timers, but only if they're programmed correctly for the season. A controller stuck on a spring schedule won't give your lawn enough water for a 101°F week.

**3. Look at drip lines for cracks or leaks.** Sun exposure breaks down plastic drip tubing over time. A cracked line loses pressure to the rest of the zone, leaving downstream plants thirsty while water pools near the leak.

**4. Confirm your Austin Water restriction stage.** [Austin Water Conservation](https://www.austintexas.gov/department/water-conservation) enforces Stage 1 through Stage 4 restrictions that control which days you can run automatic irrigation. Watering on the wrong day can mean a fine, and it won't help your landscape if you're locked out of the right schedule.

**5. Check soil moisture at root depth, not just the surface.** Push a screwdriver or soil probe 4 to 6 inches into a bed. If it's bone dry at that depth, surface watering isn't reaching the roots that actually need it.

**Key Takeaway:** A five-point irrigation check, zones, controller settings, drip lines, water restriction stage, and root-depth moisture, catches problems before they turn into dead plants or a wasted water bill.

## What to Do About New Plants and Fresh Landscaping

If you were planning to add new plants, sod, or landscape beds this week, we'd recommend holding off. New root systems haven't had time to grow deep enough to survive 100°F soil temperatures. Transplant shock combined with extreme heat is one of the fastest ways to lose an otherwise healthy plant.

This is also true for anyone considering a broader [landscape design](https://outdoorreno.com/services/landscape-design) refresh this month. We can still handle design work, material selection, and planning now, but we'll usually recommend installing new softscaping once temperatures ease. Central Texas has two solid planting windows, March through May and September through November, and both give roots a much better shot at establishing before facing extreme heat or a hard freeze.

For established landscapes, this is a good week to focus on [softscaping and planting](https://outdoorreno.com/services/softscaping-planting) maintenance instead: mulch top-offs, minor pruning of heat-stressed limbs, and monitoring native plantings like Texas sage, Lindheimer muhly, and cedar elm, all of which handle heat better than non-native turf and ornamentals. According to the [Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center](https://www.wildflower.org), native Central Texas species have evolved to handle exactly this kind of heat stress with far less supplemental water than non-native landscaping.

**Pro tip from the field:** Mulch depth matters more in a heat wave than most people realize. Kyle recommends 3 to 4 inches of shredded hardwood mulch around beds, not more. Less than 3 inches lets soil temperatures spike and invites weed seeds to germinate in the heat. More than 4 inches holds too much moisture against plant crowns, which can cause rot once storms roll through later this week. That 3 to 4 inch range is the sweet spot for Central Texas summer conditions specifically.

**Key Takeaway:** Pause new installations this week, but keep up mulch maintenance and monitor native plantings, which handle Hill Country heat far better than non-native alternatives.

## Storms Are Possible, But Don't Bank on Them

Friday through Wednesday, the forecast shows a slight to moderate chance of showers and thunderstorms. Saturday has the best odds. But "chance" is the key word. Central Texas storms in July are often scattered and unpredictable, sometimes dumping rain two miles away while your yard stays bone dry.

If your irrigation controller has a rain sensor or soil moisture sensor, make sure it's clean and working. These sensors prevent your system from running right after a storm, which saves water and avoids overwatering already-saturated black clay or limestone soils. If you're not sure whether your sensor is functioning, that's worth a quick professional look, especially heading into a week with real storm potential mixed with real heat risk.

Heavy rain risk is also part of this week's outlook. If your property has any drainage issues, low spots, erosion near a retaining wall, or water pooling near the foundation, now's the time to check those before a heavier downpour hits. Our [irrigation and drainage](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage) team handles both sides of this equation: making sure water gets to your plants when it's dry, and making sure it gets away from your house when it isn't.

**Key Takeaway:** Scattered storm chances this week don't guarantee relief. Keep irrigation running on schedule unless you can confirm meaningful rainfall, and check drainage points before any heavier downpour arrives.
