Wednesday through Friday presents the highest risk period, with temperatures dropping from 92°F to 75°F and multiple rounds of thunderstorms. **Key Takeaway:** The 20+ degree temperature drop combined with heavy rain creates perfect conditions for drainage system overload and soil movement issues common in Central Texas properties.

## Critical Drainage System Inspection

Before Wednesday's storms arrive, inspect your property's drainage infrastructure immediately. Focus on these priority areas that Kyle Stoutenger, Licensed Landscape Contractor and owner of Outdoor Renovations, identifies as most vulnerable during severe weather events.

Start with French drains and surface drainage channels. Remove debris from drain inlets, check for proper water flow direction, and ensure outlets aren't blocked. Tarrytown's mature tree canopy drops significant organic matter that can clog systems quickly.

Examine grading around your home's foundation. Water should flow away from structures at a minimum 2% slope (1/4 inch per foot). Areas where water pools against foundations during normal rain will become serious problems during heavy storm events.

Check retaining walls for proper drainage behind the structure. Inadequate drainage causes hydrostatic pressure buildup that can push walls over during saturated conditions. Weep holes should be clear and functioning.

## Irrigation System Storm Preparation

Smart irrigation controllers should be switched to storm mode or manual override before Wednesday. Most [irrigation systems in Tarrytown](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage/tarrytown) use EPA WaterSense certified controllers that can automatically adjust for weather conditions, but manual verification prevents overwatering during storm periods.

Inspect sprinkler heads for damage and ensure proper drainage around each zone. Zones with poor drainage will create waterlogged conditions that kill native plants and encourage fungal diseases. This is particularly critical for newly installed landscaping that hasn't developed mature root systems.

Test backflow preventers and shut-off valves. Storm debris can damage exposed irrigation components, and you'll need quick access to system controls if repairs become necessary during the weather event.

**Pro Tip:** Install pressure-compensating sprinkler heads if your system doesn't already have them. These maintain consistent water output even when line pressure fluctuates due to storm damage elsewhere in the municipal system.

**Key Takeaway:** Proper irrigation management during storms prevents both immediate flooding damage and longer-term plant health issues from oversaturated soil conditions.

## Protecting Hardscaping and Outdoor Structures

Secure all removable outdoor furniture, planters, and decorative elements before Monday's winds increase. Even 10-15 mph winds can move lightweight items that become projectiles during stronger storm cells.

Inspect [custom carpentry structures](https://outdoorreno.com/services/custom-carpentry/tarrytown) like pergolas, decks, and fencing for loose connections. Apply fresh sealant to any exposed wood surfaces showing weathering. Wood that's properly sealed sheds water effectively, while unsealed surfaces absorb moisture and become vulnerable to splitting and warping.

Check pavers and stone surfaces for proper joint sand levels. Low joint sand allows water penetration that can cause freeze-thaw damage (though freezing isn't expected this week) and undermines the base stability during heavy rain events.

Clean gutters and downspouts on any outdoor structures. Overflow from clogged gutters can wash out landscaping below and cause foundation issues around pergolas and covered patios.

## Plant and Tree Storm Readiness

Examine trees for dead branches, weak attachments, and signs of disease. Remove any obvious hazards before Wednesday's weather arrives, but avoid heavy pruning that creates large wound areas vulnerable to rain infiltration.

Stake newly planted trees if they haven't been in the ground for at least one full growing season. Use tree stakes rated for wind loads up to 35 mph, as embedded thunderstorms can produce stronger gusts than forecast general winds.

Mulch around tree bases helps protect roots during soil saturation and temperature swings. Apply 3-4 inch depth of quality mulch, keeping it 6 inches away from tree trunks to prevent moisture retention that encourages crown rot.

For [softscaping and planting areas](https://outdoorreno.com/services/softscaping-planting/tarrytown), ensure proper spacing and drainage. Overcrowded plant beds hold excess moisture and become disease breeding grounds during extended wet periods.

**Key Takeaway:** Focus tree preparation on removing obvious hazards rather than major pruning, which can stress trees right before challenging weather conditions.

## Emergency Drainage Solutions

Properties experiencing drainage problems during normal rain events need immediate attention before this storm system arrives. Temporary solutions can prevent major damage while permanent fixes are planned.

Install temporary surface drainage channels using sandbags or quick-setting concrete to redirect water flow away from vulnerable areas. This is particularly effective around patios and low-lying landscape areas.

Clear storm drains in streets adjacent to your property. Municipal drainage backup can cause water to sheet flow across yards, overwhelming private drainage systems that would otherwise handle the load effectively.

Consider emergency French drain installation for persistent problem areas. A properly installed French drain with 4-inch perforated pipe surrounded by #57 gravel can handle significant water volumes and provides long-term protection.

**Contractor Insight:** In Kyle Stoutenger's 16+ years of personal industry experience working across Austin Metro and Central Texas, properties with proactive drainage solutions experience 80% fewer storm damage issues compared to those relying solely on surface grading.

## Post-Storm Recovery Planning

Plan for immediate post-storm assessment once conditions are safe. Focus on structural damage first, then evaluate landscape impacts like soil erosion, plant damage, and irrigation system function.

Document any damage with photos for insurance purposes. Take images before and after the storm event when possible, as this helps establish damage causation for insurance claims.

Have contact information ready for emergency tree removal and structural repairs. Storm-damaged trees pose ongoing safety hazards and should be addressed by licensed professionals immediately.
