## Emergency Drainage Assessment Checklist

Start with your gutters and downspouts. Clean out any debris from recent winds and ensure water flows freely through the entire system. In Westlake Hills, many homes are built into hillsides, making proper gutter function critical for foundation protection.

Test your downspout discharge points. Water should flow away from structures at a minimum 2% grade – that's roughly ¼ inch drop per foot of distance. If water pools near foundations, you need immediate [drainage solutions](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage/westlake-hills) before the storms arrive.

Inspect existing French drains and channel drains. Look for standing water, clogged outlets, or damaged pipes. French drains work by collecting groundwater through perforated pipes surrounded by graded gravel. If debris has entered the system or pipes have shifted, they'll fail when you need them most.

Check swales and natural drainage channels. Remove any accumulated debris, fallen branches, or landscape materials that could create dams during heavy flow. Westlake Hills' limestone terrain means these channels carry more water volume than similar areas with more permeable soils.

Walk your property's perimeter, especially along fence lines and retaining walls. Look for areas where water might back up against structures. The alkaline limestone common in this area doesn't absorb water like other soil types, so every barrier becomes a potential flooding point.

## Professional vs. DIY Storm Prep Solutions

Some storm preparation you can handle yourself. Securing loose furniture, cleaning gutters, and removing debris from drainage channels are straightforward tasks that make immediate impact.

However, major drainage modifications require professional expertise. Installing French drains involves proper depth calculation (typically 18-24 inches in Central Texas), correct pipe sizing (usually 4-6 inch perforated pipe), and precise grading to 1% minimum slope. Get these specifications wrong, and your drainage system becomes part of the problem.

Temporary solutions can buy you time until permanent fixes are possible. Sandbags, plastic sheeting, and diversion channels work for immediate protection, but they're stopgap measures. Professional drainage systems provide long-term solutions that handle multiple storm events.

## Hill Country-Specific Challenges

Westlake Hills sits on the eastern edge of the Hill Country, where limestone bedrock creates unique landscaping challenges. Unlike the expansive clay soils found in eastern Travis County, limestone doesn't absorb water – it channels it.

This geological reality means surface drainage is everything. Water hits limestone and runs sideways until it finds a low point or artificial channel. Properties without proper grading and drainage infrastructure become collection points for neighborhood runoff.

The steep topography common in Westlake Hills accelerates water flow, increasing erosive force. A gentle rain on flat terrain becomes a torrential flow on a 15% slope. Retaining walls, terracing, and strategic placement of drainage structures become essential for property protection.

Native vegetation helps, but it's not a complete solution. Live oaks and cedar elms provide some canopy interception, but their root systems can't handle the volume of water that limestone terrain sheds during heavy rain events. Strategic planting combined with engineered drainage provides the most effective protection.

**Key Takeaway**: Westlake Hills' limestone terrain and steep slopes require specialized drainage approaches that account for rapid runoff and limited soil absorption capacity.

## Long-Term Landscape Resilience

This week's storm system highlights the importance of building weather resilience into your landscape design. Central Texas weather patterns are becoming more intense, with longer dry periods followed by heavy rainfall events.

Smart irrigation technology provides both water conservation and storm preparation benefits. Weather-based controllers from companies like Rain Bird and Hunter automatically adjust watering schedules based on rainfall forecasts. These systems can reduce water usage by 30-50% while ensuring plants aren't oversaturated before storms.

Proper [hardscaping](https://outdoorreno.com/services/hardscaping/westlake-hills) creates stable surfaces that channel water appropriately. Permeable pavers allow infiltration where soil conditions permit, while solid surfaces direct flow toward engineered drainage systems. The key is matching hardscape solutions to your property's specific topography and soil conditions.

Native plant selection reduces storm damage and maintenance requirements. Texas natives like flame acanthus and Lindheimer muhly have deep root systems that stabilize soil and flexible stems that bend rather than break in high winds. These plants also require less supplemental watering, reducing oversaturation risks.

**Key Takeaway**: Building storm resilience into your landscape requires integrated planning that combines proper drainage, appropriate plant selection, and strategic hardscape placement.
