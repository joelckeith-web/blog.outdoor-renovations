## Critical Areas to Inspect Right Now

Your foundation perimeter needs immediate attention. Lakeway's limestone substrate creates unique challenges because water either runs off too quickly or finds cracks and causes settlement issues. Check the soil grade around your foundation—it should slope away at least 1 inch per foot for the first 6 feet.

Inspect your [irrigation and drainage systems](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage/lakeway) for clogs and damage. Recent rain often reveals broken sprinkler heads, clogged drain inlets, and damaged underground lines. These problems compound during heavy rainfall events.

Look at your retaining walls and terraced areas. Hill Country properties rely heavily on these structures to manage slope drainage. Any signs of water backup, soil erosion behind walls, or structural shifting need immediate professional assessment.

Your gutters and downspouts took a beating from that 34 mph wind. Clean them now while you can safely access them. Clogged gutters during heavy rain become waterfalls that can undermine your foundation in a single storm.

## Emergency Drainage Solutions You Can Implement Now

Create temporary drainage channels using sandbags or soil berms to direct water away from vulnerable areas. This isn't a permanent fix, but it can prevent foundation damage during this week's storms.

Install temporary downspout extensions if your current system dumps water too close to your foundation. Flexible drainage pipe from any hardware store can extend your downspouts 10-15 feet from your house for immediate protection.

Clear all storm drains and drainage inlets on your property. A single clogged inlet can cause backup that floods entire areas during heavy rain. This is critical maintenance that takes minutes but prevents hours of cleanup.

Consider emergency grading in problem areas. If you have obvious low spots where water collects, you can temporarily fill these with well-draining soil or gravel. Don't use clay or organic matter—these materials will just create bigger problems when saturated.

For properties with severe drainage issues, professional [French drain installation](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage) may be necessary between storm systems. These systems collect subsurface water and redirect it to safe drainage areas.

## Long-Term Drainage Solutions for Hill Country Properties

Lakeway properties need drainage systems designed for limestone terrain and flash flood conditions. Standard suburban drainage approaches don't work in Hill Country geology.

French drain systems should be sized for 100-year flood events, not typical rainfall. This means larger aggregate, deeper excavation, and multiple outlet points. The [Texas A&M AgriLife Extension](https://agrilifeextension.tamu.edu) recommends oversizing drainage systems by 25% in karst terrain like Travis County.

Terracing and retaining walls need integrated drainage to prevent hydrostatic pressure buildup. Each terrace level should have dedicated drainage that connects to a main collection system. This prevents the cascading failures we see during heavy rain events.

Native landscaping plays a crucial role in drainage management. Deep-rooted natives like cedar elm and live oak create natural drainage channels while preventing erosion. [Native plantings](https://outdoorreno.com/services/softscaping-planting/lakeway) from the Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center's recommendations help manage both runoff and absorption.

Smart irrigation integration prevents overwatering that compounds drainage problems. [EPA WaterSense certified controllers](https://www.epa.gov/watersense) adjust for rainfall and soil moisture, preventing additional water input when soils are already saturated.

## Material Selection for Storm Resilience

Permeable hardscaping materials reduce runoff while maintaining functionality. Permeable pavers, decomposed granite, and properly graded gravel create surfaces that handle both foot traffic and storm drainage.

Drainage aggregate must be sized correctly for Central Texas conditions. We use #57 limestone for most applications—it matches local geology while providing optimal drainage flow rates. River rock looks nice but doesn't provide the angular structure needed for stable drainage beds.

Geotextile fabric prevents soil migration while allowing water flow. This is essential in Lakeway's expansive clay areas where soil movement can clog drainage systems. Proper fabric selection extends system life from years to decades.

Pipe sizing follows commercial standards even for residential applications. We install 6-inch minimum pipe diameter for main drainage lines because 4-inch pipes clog too easily with the organic debris common in Hill Country properties.

**Key Takeaway**: Material selection determines long-term performance. Use commercial-grade components sized for extreme weather events.
