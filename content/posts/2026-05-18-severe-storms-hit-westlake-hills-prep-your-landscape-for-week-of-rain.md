## What the 39 MPH Winds Already Did to Your Property

Those wind gusts over the weekend weren't strong enough to cause major structural damage, but they've created hidden problems that will become serious issues when heavy rain hits Tuesday. Here's what likely happened on your property and what you need to check today.

Fence panels and pergola connections are the most vulnerable. Wind pressure at 39 mph creates roughly 6 pounds of force per square foot on vertical surfaces. That's enough to loosen bolts, shift posts, and create gaps where water can penetrate during storms. Kyle Stoutenger, Licensed Landscape Contractor and owner of Outdoor Renovations, sees this pattern repeatedly: "Minor wind damage becomes major water damage when homeowners don't address loose connections before the next storm hits."

Your trees dropped branches and leaves that are now blocking gutters and drainage channels. Even native live oaks shed smaller branches during sustained winds, and these debris piles will turn into dams when heavy rain starts flowing. The limestone-heavy soils in Westlake Hills don't absorb water quickly, so blocked drainage creates immediate flooding problems.

**Pro tip from the field:** Walk your property with a flashlight and check every fence post for movement. Grab each post and try to wiggle it. If it moves more than a quarter-inch in any direction, the concrete footing may be cracked or the hardware needs tightening.

## Critical Drainage System Inspection Before Storms Hit

With heavy rain forecast for Wednesday through Sunday, your drainage system needs to handle multiple days of water flow without backing up. Here's your immediate inspection checklist:

**Gutters and Downspouts:** Clear all debris from gutters and test downspout flow by running water through them. Gutters should direct water at least 6 feet away from your foundation. Look for loose brackets that may have shifted during the wind event.

**French Drains and Channel Drains:** These systems are critical on Westlake Hills properties with sloped lots. Remove leaves and debris from drain grates and check that water flows freely through the system. A properly functioning French drain should handle 2-3 inches of rain per hour.

**Grading and Slope Direction:** Your property should maintain a 2% grade away from structures (2 inches of drop per 10 feet of distance). Use a string level or smartphone app to verify proper slope. The expansive clay soils in parts of Travis County can shift drainage patterns after dry periods.

**Storm Drain Connections:** Check that your property's drainage connects properly to street storm drains. Heavy rains can back up into private systems if street drains are blocked. Clear any debris from street drain grates near your property.

> **Key Takeaway:** Drainage problems that seem minor during dry weather become flooding emergencies during heavy rain events. Test your systems now while you can still make repairs before Wednesday's storms arrive.

According to [Austin Water Conservation guidelines](https://www.austintexas.gov/department/water-conservation), proper drainage design should handle a 25-year storm event (3-4 inches per hour). Most older Westlake Hills properties weren't designed to this standard and need drainage upgrades to handle modern weather patterns.

## Immediate Structural Repairs and Reinforcement

Don't wait until winds pick up again Tuesday night. Outdoor structures that survived 39 mph winds may not handle the sustained winds and rain coming this week. Here's what needs attention right now:

**Fence Panel Security:** Check every fence section for loose panels, bent posts, and damaged hardware. Wind pressure increases exponentially with speed - 50 mph winds (possible in thunderstorms) create 40% more pressure than 39 mph winds. Tighten all bolts and replace bent hardware immediately.

**Pergola and Shade Structure Inspection:** These structures catch the most wind load due to their open design. Look for loose beam connections, cracked posts, and shifted footings. Wooden structures may need additional diagonal bracing before storms intensify.

**Tree Assessment:** Focus on non-native species and any tree showing signs of lean or root exposure. [Texas A&M AgriLife Extension](https://agrilifeextension.tamu.edu) recommends removing dead branches over 2 inches in diameter before storm season. Don't attempt major tree work yourself - hire certified arborists for safety.

**Outdoor Lighting and Electrical:** Secure loose light fixtures and check that electrical connections are weatherproof. Water infiltration during storms can cause electrical hazards and expensive damage to low-voltage lighting systems.

## Emergency Erosion Control for Hill Country Properties

Westlake Hills' steep terrain makes erosion control critical during extended rain events. Here's how to protect your slopes before Wednesday's storms arrive:

**Temporary Erosion Barriers:** Install silt fencing or straw wattles along slope contours. These barriers slow water flow and catch sediment before it reaches drainage systems or neighboring properties.

**Mulch Critical Areas:** Apply 3-4 inches of mulch around tree bases and planted areas. Mulch prevents soil displacement during heavy rain. Use shredded hardwood mulch rather than pine bark, which floats away during flooding.

**Check Retaining Walls:** Inspect for cracks, bulging, or signs of water pressure buildup. Retaining walls without proper drainage can fail during sustained rain events. Look for weep holes and ensure they're not blocked.

**Protect New Plantings:** Recently installed landscapes are most vulnerable to washout. Create temporary berms using sandbags or soil to direct water around new plant installations.

## Smart Irrigation Adjustments for Storm Season

Turn off your irrigation system immediately. Running sprinklers during heavy rain wastes water, violates Austin Water restrictions, and can damage plants through overwatering. Here's how to manage irrigation during the storm period:

**Smart Controller Settings:** If you have a smart irrigation controller, verify it's connected to local weather data. EPA WaterSense certified controllers automatically skip watering cycles during rain events and can reduce water usage by 30-50%.

**Manual System Shutdown:** Turn off timer-based systems completely. Don't rely on rain sensors during severe weather - they can malfunction or get overwhelmed by wind-driven rain.

**Drainage from Irrigation Lines:** Check that irrigation system drainage valves are working. Water trapped in irrigation lines can freeze during temperature drops and cause expensive pipe damage.

**Post-Storm System Inspection:** After storms pass, inspect sprinkler heads for damage from debris and check that water pressure hasn't shifted spray patterns. Damaged heads can create wet spots that encourage fungal diseases.

> **Key Takeaway:** Proper irrigation management during severe weather protects both your landscape and your wallet. Overwatered plants are more susceptible to storm damage and disease.

## Protecting Your Landscape Investment

Quality landscaping delivers 100-200% return on investment, but storm damage can eliminate those gains in a single weather event. Here's how to protect your investment before storms intensify:

**Document Current Conditions:** Take photos of your landscape, hardscaping, and outdoor structures before storms hit. This documentation helps with insurance claims and repair planning.

**Secure Portable Elements:** Move planters, outdoor furniture, and decorative elements to protected areas. Wind-blown objects become projectiles that damage other landscape features.

**Tree Support Systems:** Young trees need additional staking during severe weather. Use flexible ties that allow some movement while preventing uprooting. Rigid staking can cause trunk damage during sustained winds.

**Cover Vulnerable Plants:** Protect newly planted or sensitive specimens with row cover fabric. Avoid plastic covers that trap heat and moisture. Remove covers as soon as storm conditions improve.

## Post-Storm Recovery Planning

Plan now for post-storm landscape recovery. Here's what you'll need to address after the weather clears:

**Drainage System Evaluation:** Professional drainage assessment after major storms identifies system weaknesses and upgrade needs. Many homeowners discover their drainage is inadequate only after flooding occurs.

**Soil Compaction Issues:** Heavy rain on Central Texas clay soils causes compaction that restricts root growth and water infiltration. Plan for soil amendment and aeration once soils dry enough for equipment access.

**Plant Disease Prevention:** Wet conditions promote fungal diseases in both turf and ornamental plants. Proper air circulation and avoiding overhead watering help prevent disease establishment.

**Hardscape Repairs:** Stone patios and walkways can shift during heavy rain events. Early repair prevents minor settling from becoming major reconstruction projects.

**Tree Health Assessment:** Storm-stressed trees need professional evaluation for structural damage, root problems, and disease susceptibility. Certified arborists can identify problems before they become hazards.

> **Key Takeaway:** Proactive post-storm planning minimizes recovery costs and prevents minor storm damage from becoming major landscape problems.
