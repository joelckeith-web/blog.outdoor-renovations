## Why Hill Country Drainage Needs Extra Attention

Central Texas soil is not like soil in most of the country. Around Westlake Hills, you're mostly dealing with thin, alkaline soil sitting on top of limestone bedrock, often called caliche. This soil has a pH that typically runs from 7.5 to 8.5, and it doesn't hold water like the deeper clay soils found in eastern parts of the metro.

Here's the problem. When caliche soil gets saturated, water has nowhere to go but sideways or downhill. Combine that with the grade changes common in Westlake Hills lots, and you get fast-moving surface water during any storm that drops more than half an inch per hour.

Texas A&M AgriLife Extension has documented how shallow, rocky soils in the Hill Country region create unique erosion and runoff challenges compared to the Blackland Prairie soils further east. That's not a generic landscaping problem. It's a Hill Country problem, and it needs Hill Country solutions.

Kyle Stoutenger, Licensed Landscape Contractor and owner of Outdoor Renovations, has seen this pattern repeat across 16+ years working Central Texas properties: a homeowner assumes their drainage is fine because it's never rained hard enough to prove otherwise. Then one storm hits, and suddenly there's a gully cut across the side yard or water pooling against the garage slab.

**Pro tip from the field:** When Kyle inspects a Hill Country property before storm season, he looks first at the transition points, where a hardscape patio meets a planting bed, or where a downspout empties onto grass. These transition zones are where 80% of erosion problems start, because water changes speed and direction right at that spot. A simple gravel splash pad or a short run of buried pipe at these transitions often prevents the erosion channel from ever forming.

**Key Takeaway:** Central Texas caliche soil and steep grades combine to move storm water fast. Erosion and flooding problems usually start at transition points, like where a downspout meets bare soil, not in open flat areas.

## Pre-Storm Checklist: What to Inspect This Week

Before Friday's rain chances arrive, walk your property and check these items. This takes maybe 30 minutes and can save you a much bigger repair bill later.

**Gutters and downspouts.** Clear leaves, seed pods, and debris. A clogged gutter sends water over the side in a sheet, right along your foundation, instead of channeling it away through the downspout where it belongs.

**French drains and channel drains.** If you have a French drain (a gravel-filled trench with a perforated pipe that redirects groundwater), check the outlet point to make sure it's not blocked by mulch, dirt, or plant growth. A French drain needs at least a 1% slope to move water by gravity. If yours was installed flat or has settled over time, it may not drain at all.

**Grading around the foundation.** Standard building code and most engineers recommend the ground slope away from your foundation at roughly 6 inches of drop over the first 10 feet. If water pools near your slab after a normal rain, that grading has likely settled or was never right to begin with.

**Retaining walls.** Look for weep holes, the small gaps near the base of a wall that let built-up water escape. If those are blocked, water pressure builds up behind the wall. That hydrostatic pressure is one of the leading causes of retaining wall failure in sloped Hill Country lots.

**Low spots and erosion channels.** Look for small ruts or bare dirt trails in your lawn. These are early signs that water is already cutting a path across your property, and it will get worse with the next hard rain.

**Key Takeaway:** A 30-minute inspection of gutters, drains, grading, and retaining walls before storms hit can catch small issues before they become expensive repairs.

## Protecting Outdoor Structures and Landscaping

Storms this week aren't just a drainage issue. Heavy rain and gusty wind can also affect wood structures, mulch beds, and newly planted landscaping.

If you have a deck, pergola, or fence installed by our [custom carpentry team](https://outdoorreno.com/services/custom-carpentry/westlake-hills), check for any loose boards or hardware before Friday. Wood that's been baking in 97 to 98°F heat all week can shrink slightly at the joints, and sudden rain plus wind can work those connections loose. It's a quick fix now versus a bigger repair after a storm rips something free.

Mulch beds also need a look. The right mulch depth is 3 to 4 inches, no more. Anything deeper holds too much moisture against plant crowns once heavy rain hits, which can lead to root and crown rot, especially in native plants like Texas sage and flame acanthus that are adapted to fast-draining, drier soil. Less than 3 inches, though, and heavy rain can wash mulch away or expose soil to erosion. If your beds are thin or bare in spots, it's worth topping them off before Saturday's rain chances increase.

Newly planted trees and shrubs from spring plantings should also get a check. Loose stakes or ties can let a young tree whip around in gusty, storm-driven wind, damaging the root ball before it's had a chance to establish. The Lady Bird Johnson Wildflower Center notes that native Central Texas species generally establish stronger root systems within the first two years, but only if they're protected from wind stress and standing water during that window.

**Key Takeaway:** Storms bring wind and saturated soil together, which stresses wood structures, mulch beds, and young trees. A quick check of hardware, mulch depth, and tree stakes now prevents bigger problems later.

## Smart Irrigation and Storm Timing

One thing a lot of homeowners forget during storm weeks: their sprinkler system. If your controller isn't smart enough to detect rain, it may keep running Monday through Thursday's dry, hot days, then continue right through Friday and the weekend's rain, wasting water and oversaturating soil that's already getting more than it needs.

EPA WaterSense-labeled smart controllers use local weather data or soil moisture sensors to skip watering cycles automatically. Central Texas homeowners typically see a 30-50% reduction in water use with a smart controller installed, according to EPA WaterSense guidance, and it also means you're not fighting Austin Water's Stage 1-4 restrictions by watering on the wrong day or during a rain event.

If you don't have a smart controller, manually pause your system through the weekend. It's a simple step, but oversaturated soil combined with storm rainfall is exactly what triggers slope failure and erosion on sloped Hill Country lots.

**Key Takeaway:** Smart irrigation controllers automatically skip watering during rain, saving water and preventing oversaturated soil. Without one, manually pause irrigation during the forecasted storm days.
