Picking the wrong plants costs money. Azaleas yellow out in three years in Austin's alkaline soil. Italian cypress snap off in spring winds. Japanese maples scorch under 100-degree heat. Meanwhile the native species that would have thrived never got chosen because they're unfamiliar.

These 30 plants all perform in Central Texas conditions — limestone or expansive clay soils, zone 8b to 9a temperatures, drought cycles, occasional hard freezes, and 100-plus-degree summers. They're organized by category so you can build a plant palette from the ground up: trees for structure, shrubs for mass, grasses for movement, perennials for color, groundcovers for finishing touches.

## Trees: The Backbone of Austin Landscapes

Trees anchor every landscape design. Six species handle Central Texas conditions and deliver decades of performance.

**Live Oak** (Quercus virginiana/fusiformis). Native, full sun, low water, 40-60 ft tall. Classic Central Texas canopy tree — holds leaves nearly year-round. Don't prune February through July (oak wilt risk).

**Texas Red Oak** (Quercus buckleyi). Native, full sun, low water, 30-40 ft tall. Compact canopy with brilliant fall color. Great for smaller lots where live oak is too big.

**Cedar Elm** (Ulmus crassifolia). Native, full sun to part shade, low-medium water, 40-60 ft tall. Tough urban tree that handles soil compaction and drought. Small leaves mean easier cleanup.

**Texas Mountain Laurel** (Sophora secundiflora). Native, full sun, low water, 15-25 ft tall. Evergreen foliage with fragrant purple spring flowers. Slow-growing specimen tree — worth the wait.

**Desert Willow** (Chilopsis linearis). Adapted from West Texas, full sun, xeric, 15-25 ft tall. Trumpet-shaped pink to purple flowers all summer, attracts hummingbirds. Fast grower.

**Anaqua** (Ehretia anacua). Native, full sun, low water, 20-40 ft tall. Evergreen in mild winters, white spring flowers, sandpaper-textured leaves. Underused Hill Country specimen.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>Key Takeaway:</strong> Trees form the backbone of Austin landscapes. Choose natives like live oak, Texas red oak, and cedar elm — not Italian cypress, Bradford pear, or Japanese maple. A single well-placed canopy tree is worth more to the finished design than a dozen shrubs.</div>

## Shrubs: Mass and Structure

Seven shrubs for the middle layer between canopy and ground.

**Texas Sage** (Leucophyllum frutescens). Native, full sun, xeric, 5-8 ft. Silvery foliage with purple flowers after rain events. Nicknamed "barometer bush." Handles everything.

**Flame Acanthus** (Anisacanthus quadrifidus). Native, full sun to part shade, low water, 3-5 ft. Orange-red tubular flowers summer through fall. Hummingbird magnet.

**Agarita** (Mahonia trifoliolata). Native, full sun to part shade, xeric, 3-6 ft. Evergreen holly-like foliage, yellow spring flowers, edible red berries. Drought-proof.

**Dwarf Palmetto** (Sabal minor). Native, part shade, low-medium water, 4-6 ft. Tropical look without tropical water needs. Great understory under oak canopy.

**Rosemary** (Salvia rosmarinus). Adapted from the Mediterranean, full sun, xeric, 3-5 ft. Culinary herb, evergreen, blue flowers winter and spring. Multiple cultivars for hedging or spreading.

**Yaupon Holly** (Ilex vomitoria). Native, full sun to shade, low-medium water, 10-25 ft. Evergreen with red winter berries. Great for hedging or as a small specimen.

**Texas Persimmon** (Diospyros texana). Native, full sun, xeric, 10-20 ft. Multi-trunk structure, smooth gray bark, small dark fruit. Refined native that works in formal designs.

## Ornamental Grasses: Movement and Texture

Native grasses give landscapes seasonal texture and handle heat better than any import.

**Lindheimer Muhly** (Muhlenbergia lindheimeri). Native, full sun, xeric, 3-5 ft. Blue-green foliage with silver-white fall plumes. The workhorse native grass.

**Gulf Coast Muhly** (Muhlenbergia capillaris). Native, full sun, low water, 3-4 ft. Pink-purple plumes in fall — dramatic mass-planting effect. Spreads; divide every few years.

**Big Muhly / Bull Muhly** (Muhlenbergia lindheimeri × capillaris). Native, full sun, xeric, 4-6 ft. Large scale for screening or backdrop planting.

**Mexican Feathergrass** (Nassella tenuissima). Native, full sun, xeric, 2-3 ft. Fine-textured foliage moves in wind. Self-seeds readily — use where spread is welcome.

**Sideoats Grama** (Bouteloua curtipendula). Native, full sun, xeric, 1-3 ft. Texas state grass, excellent for slopes and erosion control.

## Perennials and Wildflowers: Seasonal Color

Eight perennials bring color and bloom cycles that make landscapes feel alive across seasons.

**Blackfoot Daisy** (Melampodium leucanthum). Native, full sun, xeric, 8-12 in tall. White daisy flowers almost year-round in mild winters. Groundcover or border edge.

**Salvia Greggii / Autumn Sage** (Salvia greggii). Adapted from Texas-Mexico, full sun to part shade, low water, 2-3 ft. Colors from red to pink, purple, white. Blooms spring through fall.

**Damianita** (Chrysactinia mexicana). Native, full sun, xeric, 1-2 ft. Evergreen needle-like foliage, yellow flowers spring and fall. Perfect for rock gardens.

**Gregg's Mistflower** (Conoclinium greggii). Native, full sun to part shade, low water, 2 ft. Blue-purple flower clusters, butterfly magnet, spreads gently.

**Yellow Bells** (Tecoma stans). Native and adapted, full sun, low water, 4-6 ft. Yellow trumpet flowers summer through fall. Freezes back in hard winters but returns from roots.

**Rock Rose / Pavonia** (Pavonia lasiopetala). Native, full sun to part shade, low water, 3-5 ft. Pink flowers late spring through fall. Blooms nonstop.

**Four-Nerve Daisy** (Tetraneuris scaposa). Native, full sun, xeric, 8-12 in. Yellow daisy flowers almost year-round. Classic Hill Country wildflower.

**Mealy Blue Sage** (Salvia farinacea). Native, full sun, low water, 2-3 ft. Blue flower spikes late spring through fall. Handles reflective heat well.

## Groundcovers: Finishing the Ground Plane

Four groundcovers that suppress weeds and soften hardscape transitions.

**Frogfruit** (Phyla nodiflora). Native, full sun to part shade, low water. Mat-forming with white flowers, handles foot traffic. Excellent lawn alternative for small areas.

**Silver Ponyfoot** (Dichondra argentea). Native, full sun, xeric. Silver foliage trails over walls or spreads across the ground. Striking in hot dry zones.

**Horseherb** (Calyptocarpus vialis). Native, part shade, low water. Informal groundcover under oaks. Small yellow flowers. Handles foot traffic.

**Woolly Stemodia** (Stemodia lanata). Native, full sun, xeric. Silver trailing foliage with purple flowers. Replaces aggressive non-native groundcovers like Asian jasmine.

<div class="key-takeaway"><strong>Key Takeaway:</strong> A complete Austin landscape palette includes all five layers: canopy trees, understory shrubs, ornamental grasses, perennials for color, and groundcovers for the ground plane. Missing any layer creates gaps that become weed opportunities and design holes.</div>

## Plants to Avoid in Austin

These species fail regularly in Central Texas. Skip them in new installations.

**Italian Cypress** (Cupressus sempervirens). Looks great for a year or two, then gets seiridium canker and dies. Texas wind load also causes tip damage. Use columnar junipers or yaupon holly hedges instead.

**Bradford Pear** (Pyrus calleryana). Structurally weak, splits in wind, invasive in natural areas. Use Texas redbud or desert willow instead.

**Japanese Maple** (Acer palmatum). Scorches in summer heat, chlorotic in alkaline soil. Use possumhaw or Texas redbud for small understory trees.

**Azalea** (Rhododendron). Needs acidic soil — Austin's alkaline limestone is the wrong chemistry. Most fail within three years. Rethink the design rather than force the plant.

**Hydrangea** (Hydrangea macrophylla). Needs cool moist conditions and consistent water. Fails in Austin summers. Use Texas lantana or autumn sage for floral mass.

## How to Choose Plants for Your Property

The right plants depend on your specific property. Sun exposure, soil type, existing canopy, slope, and your maintenance appetite all affect the final palette.

Before buying, map your site. Where does morning sun fall? Afternoon sun? What's under the canopy and what's in open sun? Is your soil limestone (west Austin) or expansive clay (east and south)? Are there areas with poor drainage that will rot xeric plants?

Then build the palette from the top down: canopy trees first, then understory, then shrubs, then perennials and grasses, then groundcovers. Each layer should include both evergreen and deciduous species so you have year-round structure with seasonal color.
