## What to Check Right Now — Before Thursday's Storms Hit

Your 48-hour window between now and Thursday's severe weather is critical for preventing major damage. Here's what needs your immediate attention:

### Tree and Vegetation Assessment

Walk your entire property and look up. Hanging branches, split bark, and trees leaning more than before the wind event need immediate attention. Pay special attention to live oaks and cedar elms — our native species are tough, but they're not immune to wind stress.

Check your established plantings for root exposure or tilting. Wind can work root balls loose in our limestone and clay soils, especially if the ground was saturated from recent rains. Any plants that aren't sitting level need staking or replanting before the storms arrive.

### Drainage System Inspection

This is where most homeowners miss critical problems. Start with your gutters — even a small shift in alignment can cause overflow during heavy downpours. Check every downspout connection and make sure water is flowing away from your foundation, not toward it.

Walk your property's drainage patterns. Look for new erosion channels, areas where mulch has been blown away, or spots where water pools. In the Hill Country, our limestone bedrock sits close to the surface, which means water runs off fast and hard during storms.

### Hardscape and Structure Check

Examine every fence post, gate, pergola support, and retaining wall. Wind stress shows up as gaps at the base, slight tilting, or loosened hardware. Our [custom carpentry services](https://outdoorreno.com/services/custom-carpentry) team has repaired countless structures that failed during storms because wind damage wasn't addressed early.

Check patio furniture, fire pit covers, and anything else that could become projectiles in 50+ mph winds. Thursday's forecast includes thunderstorms with potentially stronger gusts than what we just experienced.

**Key Takeaway:** Every loose component becomes a weapon or a failure point during severe weather. Address problems now while you have time.

### Why Hill Country Properties Are at High Risk

Our terrain makes storm drainage particularly challenging. Steep grades common throughout the Hill Country create rapid runoff that can overwhelm standard residential drainage systems. When you combine that with our limestone bedrock — which doesn't absorb water — and pockets of expansive clay soil, you get a recipe for drainage disasters.

Properties built on hillsides or near creek beds face the highest risk. But even relatively flat lots can flood if their drainage wasn't designed for Central Texas storm patterns. ## Emergency Preparation Steps for This Week's Storms

Time is running short, but you can still take steps to protect your property. Focus on the highest-impact actions that don't require professional installation.

### Immediate Drainage Improvements

Clean every gutter and downspout on your property. Remove leaves, branches, and debris that accumulated during recent winds. Make sure water can flow freely from your roof to your yard's drainage exit points.

Extend downspouts away from your foundation if they're not already directed at least 6 feet away from your home. You can buy temporary extensions at any hardware store — they're not permanent solutions, but they'll help during this week's storms.

Check your yard's surface drainage. If water pools near your foundation, create temporary channels using a shovel to direct flow toward the street or a lower area of your property. It's not pretty, but it's effective.

### Secure Everything Loose

Based on Thursday through Sunday's forecast, winds could exceed what we experienced in the past 48 hours. Secure or store anything that could blow around: patio furniture, umbrellas, decorative planters, and especially anything near windows.

Trim any branches that are hanging loose from the recent wind event. Don't attempt major tree work yourself — call professionals for anything requiring ladders or power tools. But you can safely remove small hanging branches with hand tools.

### Turn Off Irrigation Systems

This is critical and often overlooked. Turn off your irrigation system until the storm pattern passes. The last thing your property needs during heavy rainfall is additional water from sprinklers. Over-saturation makes erosion and drainage problems much worse.

If you have a smart irrigation controller, check that it's properly connected to local weather data. Quality smart controllers can provide 30-50% water savings by automatically adjusting for rainfall, but they need to be properly programmed for Central Texas conditions.

**Key Takeaway:** Focus on drainage first, structural security second. You can't stop the storm, but you can control where the water goes.

## Understanding Central Texas Storm Patterns

Property owners who understand our regional weather patterns make better decisions about landscape protection and drainage design. Central Texas sits at the intersection of different climate zones, which creates the severe weather potential we're seeing this week.

Our average annual rainfall of about 34 inches sounds moderate, but it doesn't come evenly distributed. We get drought periods followed by intense storm seasons, often with little middle ground. This pattern is particularly hard on landscapes and drainage systems.

Spring storm seasons typically run from March through May, with a second smaller peak in September through November. The storms we're facing this week fit the classic spring pattern: rapid temperature changes, atmospheric instability, and potential for heavy rainfall rates.

### Soil Response to Storm Events

Understanding how Central Texas soils respond to heavy rain helps explain why drainage is so critical. Our expansive Houston Black clay soils absorb water slowly when dry but become impermeable when saturated. This means the first inch of rainfall might soak in, but subsequent rain runs off rapidly.

Hill Country limestone areas present different challenges. Limestone bedrock close to the surface prevents deep water infiltration, so most rainfall becomes surface runoff. Properties built on hillsides can experience runoff from upslope areas in addition to their own rainfall.

According to [Texas A&M AgriLife Extension](https://agrilifeextension.tamu.edu) research, proper drainage design must account for both soil infiltration rates and bedrock depth to be effective in Central Texas conditions.

## Native Plant Resilience During Storm Events

While we're focused on immediate storm preparation, it's worth noting how proper plant selection helps properties weather severe events. Native Central Texas plants evolved with our storm patterns and soil conditions.

Texas sage, flame acanthus, and Lindheimer muhly grass have deep root systems that help prevent erosion during heavy rain events. Live oaks and cedar elms, when properly established, can handle significant wind loads better than non-native species.

However, even native plants need proper drainage to thrive. Our [softscaping and planting services](https://outdoorreno.com/services/softscaping-planting/dripping-springs) focus on matching plant selection with site conditions, including drainage patterns and soil types.

Plants installed in areas with poor drainage often fail during storm events, not from wind or rain directly, but from root rot caused by oversaturated soil conditions. Proper site preparation and drainage are just as important as plant selection.

After this week's severe weather passes, you'll need to assess what worked and what didn't on your property. This is valuable information for preventing future storm damage.

Document any areas where water pooled, erosion occurred, or drainage systems were overwhelmed. Take photos of problem areas while the evidence is still visible. This documentation helps with both insurance claims and future drainage design.

Review your landscape's overall performance. Plants that held up well during the storm are good candidates for expansion in future plantings. Areas where plants failed might need different species or improved drainage before replanting.
