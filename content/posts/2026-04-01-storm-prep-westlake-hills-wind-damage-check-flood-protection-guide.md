## What the 37 MPH Winds May Have Done to Your Property

Those 37 mph sustained winds weren't hurricane-force, but they pack enough punch to cause problems that homeowners often miss until the next storm hits. Here's what Kyle Stoutenger, Licensed Landscape Contractor and owner of Outdoor Renovations, typically finds after wind events like this in the Westlake Hills area.

**Tree and Shrub Damage**
Wind speeds above 30 mph can crack branches on live oaks and cedar elms, especially if they were already stressed from our recent dry conditions. Look for hanging branches, splits in trunk bark, and root exposure where soil may have shifted. Native Texas sage and flame acanthus are generally wind-resistant, but check for broken stems that could become entry points for disease.

**Irrigation System Breaks**
Above-ground irrigation lines are vulnerable to wind damage, particularly where they connect to sprinkler heads or cross open areas. A single broken fitting can waste hundreds of gallons before you notice, and with Austin Water's mandatory restrictions year-round, every drop counts. Check all visible lines and look for unusually wet spots in your landscape.

**Drainage System Clogs**
Wind events fill gutters, downspouts, and French drains with debris. This seems minor until heavy rain hits and suddenly your [irrigation and drainage systems](https://outdoorreno.com/services/irrigation-drainage/westlake-hills) can't handle the flow. Properties on Westlake Hills' steep grades are especially vulnerable to backup issues.

**Key Takeaway:** Even moderate wind damage compounds quickly during heavy rain events. What starts as a small irrigation leak or partially clogged drain can become major flooding or erosion when the next storm system arrives.

## Critical Areas to Inspect Right Now

Walk your property with a systematic approach, focusing on these high-risk zones that Outdoor Renovations' team checks during post-storm assessments across Central Texas.

**Slope and Drainage Points**
Westlake Hills properties often feature dramatic elevation changes that look beautiful but create engineering challenges during storm events. Check where water naturally flows downhill and look for new erosion channels, shifted rocks, or exposed tree roots. The alkaline limestone soil common in this area can channel water unpredictably when surface layers wash away.

**Hardscape Connections**
Wind can shift paver patios, loosen retaining wall caps, and create gaps where water can penetrate behind structures. Kyle Stoutenger notes that even small separations in [hardscaping](https://outdoorreno.com/services/hardscaping/westlake-hills) can lead to major structural issues once water gets behind walls and freezes next winter.

**Roof and Gutter Systems**
Your landscape drainage system starts with your roof. Clogged gutters overflow onto landscaping and can saturate foundation areas, creating problems that extend far beyond just plant damage. Clean gutters before the storms arrive, and make sure downspout extensions direct water at least 6 feet away from your foundation.

**Outdoor Structures**
Check pergolas, gazebos, and [custom carpentry](https://outdoorreno.com/services/custom-carpentry/westlake-hills) features for loose connections. A structure that survived 37 mph winds might not handle the combination of wind and saturated soil that comes with severe thunderstorms.

## Emergency Storm Prep Steps for Westlake Hills Properties

Time is short before Thursday's first storm system arrives. Focus on these high-impact preparations that can prevent thousands of dollars in damage.

**Immediate Drainage Clearing**
Remove all debris from gutters, downspouts, and any French drains or channel drains on your property. Use a garden hose to flush systems and verify water flows freely. If water backs up anywhere, you have a clog that needs professional attention before the storms hit.

**Secure Loose Materials**
Wind and rain together create projectiles. Secure patio furniture, planters, and decorative elements. Check mulch beds and add additional material if coverage looks thin – proper mulching at 3-4 inches deep prevents soil erosion during heavy rainfall.

**Irrigation System Shutdown**
Turn off automatic irrigation systems during storm periods. Your landscape will get plenty of water naturally, and running irrigation during storms can mask broken lines and waste water when the system struggles to drain properly.

**Tree and Shrub Support**
Newly planted trees and shrubs may need additional staking before storm winds arrive. Use flexible tree ties that allow some movement while preventing complete toppling. For mature trees showing wind damage, contact a certified arborist immediately if branches threaten structures.

## Long-Term Drainage Solutions for Hill Country Properties

If this week's storms reveal drainage problems on your property, now's the time to plan permanent solutions. Outdoor Renovations has installed drainage systems across Westlake Hills for over 16 years, and certain patterns emerge on these challenging Hill Country lots.

**French Drain Systems**
Properties with clay soil components benefit from French drains that intercept groundwater before it reaches foundations or low-lying landscape areas. Proper installation requires 1-2% grade slope and specific gravel specifications that match local soil conditions.

**Terraced Retaining Walls**
Steep slopes need engineered retention to prevent erosion during heavy rainfall. Kyle Stoutenger recommends terraced systems that break up long slopes into manageable sections, each with its own drainage consideration. This approach costs more upfront but prevents catastrophic slope failure during extreme weather events.

**Permeable Hardscaping**
Traditional concrete and solid pavers contribute to runoff problems. Permeable pavers and gravel applications allow water infiltration while maintaining usable outdoor space. The EPA WaterSense program recognizes these installations as water-efficient landscape improvements.

**Key Takeaway:** Temporary fixes might get you through this week's storms, but recurring drainage problems require engineered solutions designed for Central Texas soil and weather patterns.

## Native Plant Solutions for Storm-Resistant Landscaping

While dealing with immediate storm prep, consider how your plant choices affect long-term storm resistance. Native species adapted to Central Texas weather patterns perform better during extreme events.

**Deep-Root Erosion Control**
Lindheimer muhly and Mexican feathergrass develop extensive root systems that stabilize soil on slopes. These native grasses can reduce erosion by 60-80% compared to non-native groundcovers that require more irrigation and have shallow root zones.

**Wind-Resistant Trees**
Live oaks and cedar elms, when properly maintained, withstand high winds better than fast-growing non-natives. However, proper pruning and structural support during establishment are critical. The International Society of Arboriculture recommends annual inspections for trees over 15 years old in high-wind areas.
